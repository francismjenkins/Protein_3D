Target sequence search:

A BLAST search (using UniProtKB) was conducted (keyword:enzyme AND organism:"Homo sapiens (Human) [9606]"), which returned 144 human enzymes. Only enzymes with sequence lengths between 100 and 250 residues were considered. A manual query of several proteins was conducted using BLASTP (with the database set to Protein Data Bank). The BLAST results were constrained to only sequences with percent sequence identity between 30 and 70% (using the 'formatting options' tool). The protein Maleylacetoacetate isomerase (MAAI), PDB number 1FW1, was found; with a total of 21 sequences with sequence identities within the specified range. MAAI (gene name GSTZ1) is a protein with 216 residues. It is a bifunctional enzyme able to catalyze the glutathione dependent oxygenation of dichloroacetic acid to glycoxylic acid and is a key enzyme in the metabolic degradation of phenylalanine and tyrosine. It's primary role is detoxification (of electrophilic molecules, carcinogens, and mutagens) by conjugation with glutathione.

3D Structure Prediction

Because homologous proteins exist for MAAI, we can predict its 3D structure using protein structure prediction applications. In this case we will use Swiss-Model and PHYRE2. Swiss-Model is a structural bioinformatics web-server which can be used for modeling of 3D protein structures, primarily relying on ab-initio techniques. PHYRE and PHYRE2 (Protein Homology/AnalogY Recognition Engine) predicts 3D structure based on fold recognition. PHYRE2 is coupled with 3DLigandSite, an application that enables protein binding site prediction. Another interesting feature of PHYRE2 is Backphyre, which can take a known structure and determine if there are related structures in a genome of interest (using a hidden Markov model). After modeling is completed, the structure predictions will undergo validation using the Verify3D Structure Evaluation Server, maintained by the UCLA-DOE Institute.

Swiss-Model

Swiss-Model generated a structure with Model-Template Range coverage from residue 5 through 212. The QMEAN score is -0.79. A QMEAN score near zero indicates good agreement between the model structure and experimental structures of similar size. Scores of -4.0 or below indicate poor model quality. The GMQE (or Global Model Quality Estimation) is a quality estimation which combines properties from the target-template alignment and template search method. GMQE is expressed as a number between 0 and 1 (higher numbers indicate higher reliability). The QMQE in this case is 0.98 (suggesting excellent model reliability). QMEAN is a composite scoring function which is able to derive both global and local absolute quality estimates. Below is a plot generated by Swiss-Model showing predicted local similarity to our target sequence on a per residue basis. We see that local quality remained consistently high throughout the model simulation. Below the local quality estimate plot is a Ramachandran plot. The upper left hand corner of the Ramachandran plot corresponds with beta sheets, the low left quadrant corresponds with right-handed alpha helices, and the top right quadrant with left handed alpha helices. The lower right quadrant has almost no outlined region. The conformation in this region (psi around -180 to 0 and phi around 0 to 180 degrees) is disfavored due to steric clash. Generally, it plots psi against phi angles. Residues about the omega bond lack rotational freedom (due to its double bond); however, the psi and phi bonds are single bonds emanating from the alpha carbon and are therefore free to rotate. In our plot below, most of the points appear in the darker colored regions, which are the regions where the bond angles are most easily allowed. We do see several points in lighter colored regions, which are less favorable regions , and we see two points in the disallowed region of the plot. This may suggest a minor problem with the model.

Phyre2

Like other remote homology recognition applications, Phyre2 uses protein threading to create its model. Because protein structure is more conserved in evolution than its amino acid sequence, a protein can be modeled with reasonable accuracy on a distantly related sequence of a known structure. Phyre2 provides a template of domain matches color coded by confidence level. In this case, Phyre2 was able to provide coverage of 98% of the sequence (212 out of 216 residues), with 120 templates, ranging from a low of about 15% sequence identity to a high of 83%, each with 100% confidence. In the secondary structure and disorder prediction section of the report, Phyre2 indicates that 53% of the structure are alpha helices, 10% beta strands, 15% transmembrane helix, and 9% disordered. Interestingly, some isomerases associate with biological membranes as either peripheral membrane proteins or through a single transmembrane (TM) helix (although it's not clear that this is the case here). Phyre2 predicts two relatively small transmembrane regions. One between residues 100 and 115, and the other between residues 157 and 173 (each composed of a single TM helix).

Mutation model

An amino acid substitution was introduced at position 99. The wild-type protein has a Valine at position 99. AUTO-MUTE was used to predict activity and stability changes resulting from a V99M mutation. A novel V99M from a single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) has been associated with an extremely slow rate of dichloroacetate (DCA) clearance. DCA is a drug used to treat lactic acidosis and solid tumors. AUTO-MUTE predicted that protein activity is unaffected by the mutation (with a confidence level of 0.54); however, protein stability is decreased (with a confidence level of 0.95). Interestingly, valine is relatively close to methionine in terms of mutational difference, and they are both small hydrophobic residues similar in physio-chemical properties. Position 99 exists at the interface between the extracellular and transmembrane section of the protein (this is according to the Phyre2 prediction, and please note --this prediction may be problematic). Overall, it was somewhat surprising that AUTO-MUTE did not predict activity changes but did predict stability changes (given the extent to which activity can change with a mutation at position 99, and the physio-chemical similarity between the native and substitute amino acids). Recall, one of the most important roles of GSTs is detoxification. These enzymes predominantly express in the liver, and they're responsible for clearance of many different types of chemical toxins. This particular mutation plays a role in DCA induced inactivation of GSTZ1 (a process which has been fairly well studied). DCA is a mechanism-based inactivator of GSTZ1 (i.e. DCA elimination is slowed with repeat dosing). Thus, this mutation interferes with the detoxification function of GSTZ1 (it's primary function).

However, perhaps the most interesting information uncovered during this project is the disparity in domain characterization between the different prediction models, discussed below.

Evaluation of binding site

The image shown below is from the results of the Swiss-Model prediction for the wild-type enzyme and (below the Swiss-Model data) an image taken from a BLAST search conducted on UniProtKB. As shown, there are several predicted binding sites within the enzyme. However, there appears to be some problems when comparing the predictive models to the BLAST search. Namely, BLAST reports binding sites at positions 45, 59, and 111 and Phyre2 predicts the transmembrane portion of the enzyme lies between residues 100 and 115 (and another between residues 157 and 173). One possibility is non-covalent binding to membrane lipids. However, BLAST describes this as a Glutathione binding site. Position 111 is occupied by Glutamine, and while Glutamine is hydrophobic, and in general, segments with a high degree of hydrophobicity are required to span a lipid bilayer, a TM region generally needs to be 20-30 amino acids long to cross the entire plasma membrane (and Phyre2 predicts TM regions of 15 amino acids long). In an effort to investigate functional changes that may be caused by mutation, several mutations of position 111 were investigated using AUTO-MUTE. The glutamine was replaced with an amino acid from each class of amino acids (e.g. polar, non-polar, positive/negative charge); and none of the substitutions were able to predict activity change.



It should be noted, Swiss-Model also predicts a protein-ligand interaction site at position 111. Below is an image taken from Swiss-Model of an enlarged image of position 111 and its surrounding region. The glutamine is represented by the section of ribbon highlighted in red.



To elucidate on whether GSTZ1 contains a transmembrane domain, TMHMM (a transmembrane prediction server using a hidden Markov model) was used to predict the existence or non-existence of transmembrane domains. As shown, TMHMM predicts no transmembrane domains in GSTZ1:


However, MEMSAT (a program which uses support vector machines to predict the secondary structure and topology of all-helix TM proteins), like Phyre2, predicts that the region in question (residues 100 to 115) is a transmembrane region. Some members of the cytosolic GST superfamily have been shown to form ion channels in intracellular membranes, and GSTZ1 is a cytosolic GST (although it can also locate to the mitochondria). It is also important to note that there are several transcript variants of GSTZ1 which encode multiple protein isoforms. Ab-initio, or de novo modeling seeks to build 3D protein structures from scratch, but it's a very broad term, and many different approaches (or combinations thereof) can be described as ab initio. In general, an initial effort is made to predict secondary structures using physio-chemical properties (and tools such as neural networks), then algorithms attempt to predict tertiary folding. Fold recognition methods, on the other hand, rely on a library of known protein folds. This helps explain the different results across different platforms. An interesting follow up to this project might be to look at a set of proteins that each have several isoforms and compare the quality of different structure prediction approaches.

Conclusion

We were able to see a real example of the distinctions between different protein structure prediction models, and improve our understanding of why these differences exist, which will hopefully improve our ability to select an ideal model in real world applications.
